Warp Home

Megan Ball, Rachel Brooks, Alex Hogue and Sydney Meza

Overview

For this project, we worked with Warp to design a home page based on their project brief. This was an amazing opportunity to learn about designing with a team and complete the iterative design process (sketches, low fidelity wireframe, and high fidelity prototypes). We also received feedback on our wireframe from both students in our class and David, a Senior Product Designer at Warp, in order to improve our design.

Project Brief

Warp is a customizable terminal with collaborative tools and AI integration. To summarize the project brief, Warp does not have a centralized home page for users to view information about their files, team, usage, account and learn more about Warp. Currently, a lot of this information is in the settings page, which makes it difficult to find. The home page that we designed will serve as an aggregator that ultimately allows for increased discovery and usage of Warp’s features. Design Decisions

One major design decision we made was regarding the content to display on the home page. While we had a list of considerations from the project brief, it wasn't feasible to include everything. Drawing inspiration from Figma Home, Google Drive and GitHub home, we opted to merge the current drive on Warp into our new home pages. This approach enhances how intuitive the app is to use and reduces time spent searching for desired content. Additionally, we deliberated on how to present metrics, live sessions, activity, and Warp content. Centralizing this information on a left hand sidebar, we aimed for visual appeal by using profile icons for team members, filled bars for metrics, and link icons to indicate that clicking on the Warp resources would redirect users. Furthermore, we considered what user states to design for. It was crucial to cater to both new users (requiring more instructional guidance) and active users (with their current content readily accessible).

Inspiration From Sketches

We noticed that many of us organized content using sidebars and a topbar. Therefore, we opted to create a left-sidebar (which we noticed was an industry standard compared to a right-sidebar) and added a welcoming header for a personal touch. We selected the most-commonly found modules from our sketches to include in the left sidebar. We also wanted the homepage to be customizable after noticing this trend amongst the sketches. Thus, we allow users to hide individual modules and the left sidebar if desired. Moreover, many of us had separate team and individual pages, so we discussed how a user could navigate between these, ultimately deciding upon a toggle. Finally, we found that visualizing the items (workflows, sessions and notebooks) as boxes, rather than in a list, made the interface feel less crowded/overwhelming.

Intended Impact

Our main goal with the home page was to create a user-friendly, glanceable interface that allows users to understand their current state and easily access their content. We achieved this through a split layout: the left sidebar displays metrics, live sessions, activities, and resources, while the main section shows folders, files, and sessions. Additionally, since Warp is designed for collaboration, it was important for users to seamlessly switch between personal and team views, which organizes their content while still keeping team information accessible. Ultimately, our design aligns with the priorities outlined in our project brief and enhances the discovery of Warp’s features by moving key functionalities from the settings page to the home page.

Feedback on Wireframes

We created a five minute presentation of our wireframe using Loom (it is addressed to David), which you can watch here.

Below is some feedback that we received about our first iteration wireframe. It is color-coded based on who gave the feedback: red = David, blue = Vanessa, green = class.

  • If a user is on multiple teams, then a UI may not be the best design choice. (David also clarified that while Warp currently only allows a user to be on one team, in the future, a user will be able to join multiple teams.)
  • It is unclear how to get from the current view to the terminal and how to get from the terminal to this view.
  • It is unclear where the black arrow on the top left of the screen goes back to.
  • The wireframe currently does not show how to manage (invite and remove) members in the team as well as change permissions, which is important for the team page.
  • It is unclear where an object would be added if a user clicked the plus sign.
  • You should ensure that the order of modules on the left navigation bar is based on what is most important to the user.
  • The layout feels similar to Google Drive or Finder, which will make it intuitive for users to navigate.
  • Having too many items in recents may defeat the point of an aggregator. In addition, people typically do not interact with a side scroll, so it may be unnecessary.
  • It would be beneficial to show what happens when you open a folder.
  • Rather than displaying all the items, folders and sessions on the home, you can only show a few and then have a “view more” button that opens another page.
  • People may want to have more control over how they see the items in their drive. For instance, you could have an option to toggle between list and gallery mode, filters and sorting.

Second Iteration Wireframes

These are our second iteration wireframes, which were revised based on the feedback that we recieved:

(include images of second iteration wireframes)

We felt that having four screens was too few to show user interaction with the various components, so we created more screens in our second iteration. We removed the metrics from the left sidebar since metrics was last on our priorities list, and it did not feel necessary for users to constantly see this information (better in the account page). Moreover, we received a lot of feedback about making the drive more customizable and realized that we were missing basic features, like trash and a search bar. Therefore, in addition to including these features, we added sorting, filters, and two modes for viewing. We also decided not to extend the recent items beyond the page because having both horizontal and vertical scrolling, especially when the user has many items in their drive, could be confusing. Additionally, we changed the toggle (navigating between “Personal” and “Team”) to a drop-down since David clarified that in the future Warp would allow users to be on multiple teams. Finally, we wanted to make it easy to navigate between the home page and terminal. We opted to use the same top bar as the Warp app, but added a new tab for the home page, replacing the drive tab since the drive is integrated into the home.

We incorporated most of the feedback in our second iteration, except we didn't find it necessary to restrict the drive content on the home screen. Our decision was based on the concern that limiting content would increase the number of clicks needed for users to access what they want. Furthermore, users can organize their items into folders if they feel they have too many.
